  <!-- SECTION 1: WHAT IT IS -->
  <section class="content-section">
    <h2>What it is</h2>
    <p>
      BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound-157) is a synthetic pentadecapeptide, a chain of 15 amino acids, derived from a partial sequence of a protective protein found in human gastric juice. It does not occur in this exact form naturally; it was isolated and synthesized by researcher Predrag Sikiric and colleagues at the University of Zagreb, with research beginning in the early 1990s.<a href="#ref-1" class="cite">[1]</a>
    </p>
    <p>
      It is sometimes referred to as PL 14736, PL-10, or simply "body protection compound." It is not a growth hormone, hormone secretagogue, or steroid analog; it belongs to a distinct class of gastroprotective peptides. As of this writing, BPC-157 has not been approved by the FDA for any indication, though it has been used in human clinical trials for inflammatory bowel conditions.<a href="#ref-1" class="cite">[1]</a>
    </p>
  </section>

  <!-- SECTION 2: RESEARCH AREAS -->
  <section class="content-section">
    <h2>What researchers study it for</h2>
    <ul class="research-list">

      <li>
        <strong>Gut health and mucosal integrity</strong>
        <span>BPC-157 was originally studied in the context of gastric protection and inflammatory bowel disease; preclinical models have examined its effects on gut lining repair, ulcer healing, and mucosal homeostasis, and it has been investigated in human ulcerative colitis trials.<a href="#ref-1" class="cite">[1]</a></span>
      </li>

      <li>
        <strong>Tendon and ligament healing</strong>
        <span>Multiple preclinical studies have examined BPC-157's influence on tendon repair, finding evidence of increased tendon outgrowth and improved cell survival and migration in both in vitro and animal models.<a href="#ref-5" class="cite">[5]</a><a href="#ref-2" class="cite">[2]</a></span>
      </li>

      <li>
        <strong>Muscle repair</strong>
        <span>Animal model studies have investigated BPC-157 in the context of muscle crush injuries and transection wounds, consistently reporting accelerated recovery compared to controls.<a href="#ref-2" class="cite">[2]</a></span>
      </li>

      <li>
        <strong>Wound healing and skin repair</strong>
        <span>A 2021 review in <em>Frontiers in Pharmacology</em> examined BPC-157's role in skin wound therapy, including incisional wounds, excisional wounds, and burns, alongside its proposed effects on vascular repair and angiogenesis.<a href="#ref-4" class="cite">[4]</a></span>
      </li>

      <li>
        <strong>Joint health and cartilage</strong>
        <span>A small retrospective clinical series published in 2021 looked at intra-articular BPC-157 injection for multiple types of knee pain, including osteoarthritis; 14 of 16 patients reported pain relief, though the study's small size limits conclusions.<a href="#ref-6" class="cite">[6]</a></span>
      </li>

      <li>
        <strong>Orthopaedic and sports medicine applications</strong>
        <span>A 2025 systematic review in <em>HSS Journal</em> assessed preclinical evidence across fractures, tendon ruptures, ligament tears, and muscle injuries, concluding that the existing animal model data is consistently positive but that human trials are still needed.<a href="#ref-3" class="cite">[3]</a></span>
      </li>

    </ul>
  </section>

  <!-- SECTION 3: RESEARCH CONTEXT -->
  <section class="content-section">
    <h2>Research context</h2>
    <p>
      The BPC-157 evidence base is unusually large for a research peptide, with over 100 published studies, but the vast majority are preclinical, conducted in rodent models by a small number of research groups, predominantly the Sikiric group at the University of Zagreb.<a href="#ref-1" class="cite">[1]</a> The consistency of findings across multiple injury types and animal models is notable, and independent research groups have replicated several key findings.<a href="#ref-2" class="cite">[2]</a><a href="#ref-3" class="cite">[3]</a>
    </p>
    <p>
      Human clinical data is limited but not absent. BPC-157 has been studied in human Phase II trials for ulcerative colitis and multiple sclerosis, with no reported toxicity and no LD1 established in animal safety studies.<a href="#ref-4" class="cite">[4]</a> The 2025 systematic review by Vasireddi et al. concluded that while preclinical evidence supports further investigation, there are currently no completed large-scale randomized controlled trials in humans for musculoskeletal applications.<a href="#ref-3" class="cite">[3]</a> Researchers interested in this peptide should weigh the strength of the animal model evidence against the limited human data.
    </p>
  </section>
